2. Brand Voice & Writing Guidelines
**Max 900 words** for blog posts and thought leadership.
Always
- Use complete sentences and natural paragraph flow
- Vary sentence structure and length
- Write in a conversational yet professional tone
- Connect ideas with transitional phrases rather than relying on formatting
- Focus on practical insights and real-world applications
- Maintain technical accuracy and credibility
- Be specific (numbers, names, timeframes)
Never
- Use em-dashes — use periods, commas, or parentheses instead
- Overuse bullet points — prefer prose; use bullets sparingly for quick reference
- Use “revolutionary,” “seamless,” “game-changing,” or “innovative”
- Hedge (“I think maybe…”)
- Use distancing language (“The company…” vs. “We…”)
- Make generic statements
- Never refer to Arcade as a platform. Arcade is a runtime
 
3. LinkedIn — Alex (Founder Account)
**Word Count:** 150-350 words (max 500 for technical breakdowns) **Tone:** Founder POV, technical authority, challenger energy **Posting Windows:** 8-10am PST (announcements) or 3-5pm PST (narratives)
 
Post Types That Work
1. Story-First (Highest Engagement)
Opens with a real conversation or experience. Uses pain to set up the insight.
**Best for:** Auth pain, production failures, customer conversations
**Example hooks:** - “I just got off a call with…” - “True story: an Enterprise team spent 6 weeks…” - “Last week someone showed me their AI agent demo…” - “I watched another team abandon their MCP project…”
2. Architecture Breakdown (High Saves)
Breaks down why something technical is harder than it seems.
**Best for:** API complexity, tool-calling patterns, protocol explanations
**Example hooks:** - “The Gmail API doesn’t have a ‘reply to email’ endpoint. Let that sink in.” - “That’s 5 opportunities for hallucination. 5x the tokens. 5x the latency.”
3. Contrarian Take (High Comments)
Challenges conventional wisdom with a clear, defensible position.
**Best for:** Industry trends, common mistakes, reframing problems
**Example hooks:** - “Hot take: Most SQL agents shouldn’t actually write SQL.” - “Hot take: Agents are just apps.” - “Stop converting your REST APIs to MCP.”
4. Industry News + POV
Reacts to news/trends with a unique founder perspective.
**Best for:** Press coverage, ecosystem updates, competitor moves
**Example hooks:** - “The MCP ecosystem just hit a major inflection point this week.” - “[Company] just announced X. Here’s what everyone’s missing…”
 
Winning 5-Part Structure
1. Tension Hook (1-3 lines visible before “see more”)
LinkedIn truncates posts after ~3 lines. The hook must stop the scroll and earn the click.
Our approach: **Curious, Authentic, Genuine.** Not clickbait or manufactured outrage.

2. Story/Context (2-4 sentences)
Ground the hook in reality. Reference real conversations, teams, or experiences with specific details.
3. Technical Breakdown (3-6 bullet points or short paragraphs)
Explain WHY the problem exists. Show the underlying architecture. Be specific about technical details.
4. Broader Statement (1-2 sentences)
Zoom out to the industry implication. Connect to a larger trend. Sound like earned wisdom — not a product pitch.
5. Soft CTA (1 sentence)
Invite engagement without being salesy.
**Strong CTAs:** - “Anyone else hitting this auth wall?” - “What’s your approach?” - “Are you building for demos or production?” - “Link in comments.”
**Avoid:** “Try Arcade today!” or direct product pitches.
Arcade Company Tag (Required)
Always include one soft mention of Arcade.dev — embedded naturally in a sentence, never as a standalone pitch.
**Natural placement options:** - “At Arcade.dev, we learned…” - “This is why we built Arcade.dev.” - “We hit this wall deploying thousands of production tools at Arcade.dev.”
 
Formatting Rules
- Use single newlines between thoughts — never dense paragraphs
- Short 1-3 sentence blocks only
- **Rhetorical devices that work:** Contrast pairs (“Not a model problem. An architecture problem.”), rhetorical questions, mini-reveals, short sentences as standalone paragraphs (“That’s it.”)
- Alternate link placement — sometimes “link in comments,” sometimes inline in the post
 
Voice Guidelines
**Do:** - Sound like a founder who builds, not a marketer who promotes - Use “I” and “we” naturally - Share hard-earned lessons - Be direct and opinionated - Challenge the industry - Show technical depth
**Don’t:** - Use corporate speak - Sound promotional in the hook - Use em-dashes - Say “revolutionary,” “seamless,” “game-changing” - Over-explain Arcade

4. LinkedIn — Ben Sabrin (GTM Executive)
**Word Count:** 100-250 words **Tone:** Grounded, credible, practical, customer-centric **Posting Windows:** 8-10am PST
 
Ben’s Voice Principles
**Do:** - Stay grounded in reality — speak from actual customer conversations - Be measured — one clear point, well-made - Lead with credibility — decades of GTM experience is the hook - Be practical — give people something actionable - Celebrate others — give kudos to teammates, customers, partners by name - Ask real questions — not rhetorical, genuinely curious
**Don’t:** - Over-dramatize — no “made my stomach drop” moments - Exaggerate scale — avoid “millions of times” or “everyone is doing X” - Sound like marketing — no hype words, no manufactured urgency - Be theatrical — save the drama for the founder account - Claim universal truths — speak from your experience, not absolutes
 
Post Types That Work
1. Customer Insight (Primary)
Share something specific you learned from a customer conversation.
Pattern: What you heard / Why it matters / What it means for others
Example opener: “Talked to a CTO this week who said something I keep thinking about: [specific quote].”
2. Industry Observation (Grounded)
Notice a pattern — keep it practical and specific.
Example opener: “I’ve had three conversations this month where [specific thing] came up. Curious if others are seeing this too.”
3. Team/Customer Win
Celebrate a win, but make it about others.
Example opener: “[Company] just went live with [thing]. Huge credit to [person] who [specific contribution].”
4. Practical Tip
Share something tactical from GTM experience.
Example opener: “Something that’s worked well in enterprise sales conversations lately: [specific tactic].”
5. Connection/Event
Reference a meeting, event, or relationship.
Example opener: “Good conversation with [person/company] yesterday about [topic].”
 
Hook Guidance
Ben’s hooks should feel like the start of a business conversation, not a LinkedIn performance.

Pre-Publish Checklist (Ben)
- Would Ben actually say this out loud to a customer?
- Every claim grounded in specific experience (not sweeping)?
- No dramatic hooks or manufactured tension?
- Under 250 words?
- One clear point, not five?
- If celebrating, is someone else getting the credit?
