### Details
- **Focusing on Workflow and Future Projects**: The discussion centered on completing outstanding "random stuff" to focus on Single Sign-On (SSO) the following week, noting that the team is "almost through" a period of less critical tasks. The success of the "work in progress" (WIP) process was noted, as team members like Pascal and Joe have successfully picked up useful tasks from the speaker's plate, which is seen as "perfect". The plan is to solidify the monorepo structure and then focus on more critical items like SSO.
- **Discussion on Meeting Structure and Backlog Management**: The participants debated the best approach for these meetings, considering reviewing the backlog and planning, or using the time to force-rank priorities to ensure the most important items are at the top. They also agreed that it is important to capture fire drills correctly and ensure that cross-team dependencies are addressed. The decision was made to check the ordering of items in all columns to uphold the team contract that idle members should work on the highest-priority task.
- **The Importance of Tool Composability**: Tool composability was identified as a critical and recurring topic, driven by customer needs to mix tools, such as combining a Salesforce tool with custom elements for high-order workflow tools. The conversation highlighted that this feature must accommodate tools hosted internally versus remote gateways, implying the need for remote calls and governance checks at each step. The group noted the complexity of implementing deterministic, transactional workflows with potential rollbacks, which differentiates it from non-deterministic "skills".
- **Tool Composability as a Workflow Builder**: The participants acknowledged that the request for tool composition is essentially a demand for a workflow builder, similar to what Sergio had previously shown, which leans toward deterministic, code-based solutions. The Office TV Robot noted that building a workflow engine based on RPC calls, while interacting with their Multi-Context Platform (MCP) for governance and tracking, takes the company into territory occupied by iPaaS platforms like Merges or NADN. This move represents a strategic decision to push determinism further into their offerings.
- **Project Review and Priority Check**: The primary focus for the meeting was set as going through current projects and then reviewing Evan Tahler's paternity plan. The team opted to focus primarily on planning and ensuring the priority order is correct, rather than extensively reviewing "in progress" items, unless there was "shadow work" that needed to be captured. The urgency of deployment was discussed, starting with deployment of code toolkits as a necessary step for enabling external developers.
- **Prioritizing Arbback over Toolkit Deployment**: The team agreed that the **Arbback** initiative should take precedence over toolkit deployment because it is a more significant blocker for customers, while toolkit deployment, though annoying, primarily affects internal teams. The current status of **Arcade deploy for on-prem customers** was confirmed to be at the bottom of the planning list, as Morgan Stanley does not require it immediately, although Stitch Fix might. It was clarified that "Planning" implies someone is actively writing a project plan.
- **Azure Program Qualification and Requirements**: A new high-priority initiative involves qualifying for a critical Azure program that promises enterprise customer pitches and funding. A key requirement is making Arcade easily purchasable via the Azure app store, but confusion remains over the need to run their infrastructure in Azure, specifically concerning control planes and data planes. The team, particularly the infrastructure-minded members, will need to determine if a full Azure production environment is required or if a preview of the on-prem deployment would suffice.
- **Tool Search and Productization Timeline**: A concern was raised that the cloud productization of tool search was ranked low in the backlog. It was acknowledged that putting this component far down suggests an expectation of slow progress, although the serialized view of the backlog does not preclude picking up multiple items. The team confirmed ongoing work on tool search, with Joe recently starting an initial component (merge the "s***").
- **Ronaldo's Shift in Development Strategy**: Ronaldo's approach to development has changed; they will now try to use "cloud code primitives" first, rather than immediately building from "starter kits". This philosophy is seen as a positive outcome of adopting Cloud Code as the foundation, allowing them to leverage existing rules, hooks, and skills instead of developing a full agent harness or a Model Context Workflow (MCW).
- **Sequencing Tool Composability and Typed Tool Responses**: The conversation shifted to the priority between **Tool Composability** and **Typed Tool Responses** for both programmatic and UI-based workflow builders. It was argued that Typed Tool Responses, which reveal the expected output before execution, are critical for making tool composability good and ensuring non-destructive actions can be composed. The larger strategic and planning lift associated with Tool Composability, despite being potentially less complex to code, justifies keeping it at a higher priority to start the necessary planning earlier.
- **Review of MCP Server Discoverability**: The idea of making first-party Multi-Context Platform (MCP) servers available and discoverable was discussed, with the realization that Guru's toolbench project addresses the "quality information" aspect through an external website. The team decided to move the internal discoverability initiative to the "ice box," assuming that Guru's external toolbench and simple in-UI pre-filled dropdown lists would suffice for now.
- **Front-End Engineering Management and Organization**: The structure of engineering management during Evan Tahler's absence was discussed, with Sam K being assigned to manage both the new Tools and DX teams. The plan is to split the existing Tools and DX team into separate Tools and DX teams, with Wills serving as the interim tech lead for the DX team, where they will hire their permanent replacement. This reorganization clarifies roles and addresses the need for a long-term charter focusing on onboarding and user experience.
- **Management and Vendor Processes During Absence**: Evan Tahler outlined process expectations for their absence, asking that project reviews continue to be followed, including the new requirement for all medium-to-large front-end changes to include a mockup and API design approved by Sergio and Teal. Sterling and Nate were designated to manage any new vendor selections, with a preference for Amazon-provided or open-source hosted versions first. The need to quickly socialize the new organizational plan with the rest of the team was emphasized.
- **Strategic Product Direction and Initiative Stack Rank**: A key strategy item was deferring product management decisions to Salazar until an Enterprise Product Manager is hired, to prevent engineers from becoming overextended. Evan Tahler provided a stack rank of initiatives for Nate, prioritizing finishing the enterprise table, launching tool search, addressing notification/prompt pass-through, and beginning work on the **Distributed File System** (context boxes). For the other team, the stack rank prioritized hand-coding enterprise tools, spinning up the tool factory (agent development and deployment), and then tool composability.
- **Review of Role Transitions and Hiring Status**: Evan Tahler reviewed the status of open positions, noting that the 'head of product' role is the most sensitive one on the list, and they should place question marks on its status. They confirmed that the hiring plans and job descriptions, including detailed explanations of interview goals, are available via a shared link.

Cassie will manage a pool of recruiters to help hire for roles such as the marketing and engineering positions.
