## Social Content Strategy

This is a living doc. These are patterns that emerged from doing the work, not a pre-defined strategy. If something here feels wrong, update it with what you learned and why.


### Why Social Mattered

The metric was signups. The theory was simple: if people don't know about us, they can't sign up. If we get them to the site, the most qualified users will convert. Everything else (followers, impressions, referral traffic) was a leading indicator of that.

The audience was developers, but it was emergent. We intentionally tried to separate the PLG motion from enterprise sales. What we found was different: developer-facing content was generating enterprise pipeline directly. Teams or execs trying to solve a problem would find our content, play around with the product, and reach out for help or enterprise plan info. Social wasn't just awareness -- it was how enterprise buyers were finding us.

That discovery shifted the company toward an enterprise motion. But the implication for content strategy is important: the developer content that drove PLG and the content that generated enterprise interest were the same content. We didn't need different funnels. We needed consistent distribution of useful, technical content.


### What We Did (April - November 2025)

A one-to-two person effort ran distribution across 8 channels for roughly 6 months (with YouTube and X mostly eventually handed off to Mateo, who took over posting there).

Content pillars:
1. Art of the possible -- demos, workflows, "look what you can build"
2. Agent auth and MCP -- the core technical differentiation
3. Individual integrations -- specific tool guides, use cases
4. Thought leadership -- founder/CTO perspectives on the AI agent space

In practice, content was reactive to what engineering shipped and what the ecosystem was doing. The pillars were rough guardrails, not an editorial calendar. When volume was high enough (~20-30 posts/week across channels), all four got covered naturally.


### Channel Strategy: Why These Channels, What We Learned

#### **LinkedIn (primary channel)**

LinkedIn worked because the effort-to-impact ratio was the best of any channel. Outsized impressions and referral traffic relative to the content being produced.

The strategy was people as the nexus, brand as second layer. Alex's personal account was the primary growth driver. Arcade company account growth correlates directly to Alex's account growth -- when Alex posted, the brand page grew too. We tried to build up Sam and Ben's accounts independently but getting consistent content from them was difficult. Pivoted to using their accounts to amplify Alex's posts through comments, reposts, and quote-posts, which worked better.

LinkedIn was responsible for 1,300-1,500 monthly visitors to arcade.dev, making it the #1 social traffic source.
#### ****
#### **YouTube**

Strategy was modeled on a friend's channel that grew exponentially: long-form interviews as the anchor, then cut shorter clips to maintain consistency between uploads. Layered in standalone Shorts aimed at stirring up emotion or interest and pointing back to the original video.

Consistency mattered most here, followed by outside distribution. One key learning: content that was immediately distributed outside YouTube would spike in views but often peter out after. We shifted to letting the YouTube algorithm work for 2-3 days first, then distributing wider. Best external distribution channels for YouTube content were Reddit and LinkedIn.

YouTube subscriber growth continued during the low-cadence period, but that's because an agency (Rise) took over posting. The results were notably worse despite similar publishing volume:
- Subscriber growth rate dropped 3x (81/mo to 27/mo)
- The top single video from the in-house period generated more subscribers (144) than the entire agency period combined (109)
- Click-through rates fell from 5-8% to 1-4%, suggesting titles and thumbnails were less resonant with the target audience
- Watch hours per video dropped significantly, which affects YouTube's algorithmic promotion of subsequent content
- Content that performed best was niche-specific (MCP, auth, agent frameworks) rather than broad (model reviews, AI news)

The takeaway: publishing volume alone doesn't sustain a channel. The in-house content worked because it was specific to our ICP and the creative direction came from in-house, not because there was more of it.

#### **Reddit**

The strategic value of Reddit is distribution + SEO/AIO, not community. Posts are sticky and long-lived (unlike LinkedIn/X where content dies in 24-48h), and a good post on the right subreddit becomes a soft entry point when someone (or an LLM) searches for that topic. A single top post can clear 10k+ impressions.

We went through two phases: first posting from an ancillary perspective (pretending to be adjacent to Arcade), then switching to a transparently Arcade-branded account. Transparency was better -- less risk, less stress, and Reddit users respect honesty more than they respect stealth marketing.

The hard part was emotional, not tactical. Negative comments felt like brand damage, which made it rough to post consistently. The stakes felt higher than they probably were, but the negativity made it difficult to sustain. We also got a short-term account lock for coordinated upvotes from the same IP range (lesson learned: never share Reddit posts internally for amplification).

Content that worked: educational blog posts, YouTube videos, data-driven posts ("testing 4k tools on Anthropic tool search"), and memes. Anything that read like a press release or product announcement got buried. We never fully solidified channel strategy here because ICP and product messaging were still a moving target, and we were spread across too many channels.

#### **TikTok**

Greenfield -- no competitors were on TikTok, which made it appealing. But the platform is fundamentally difficult for B2B:
- TikTok rewards content consistency (same person, same type of content), not just posting frequency. Impressions and scrolls are easy to get, but the platform disincentivizes following.
- Very locked-in. Hard to drive people off-platform. Lead-gen functionality was restrictive (essentially only for selling ebooks or similar) and the platform was strict about external links.
- Our strategy was to repost YouTube Shorts. They sort of performed, but not consistently enough to justify the time.

If we tried again, it would need a dedicated strategy with different asset types designed for TikTok's lead-gen functionality, probably aligned with Val and growth product initiatives. Not worth doing halfway.

#### **X (Twitter)**

Similar dynamic to LinkedIn: the companies that "do well" on X are using people as the nexus, with company accounts as a second layer. Unless you want to go the Duolingo route (which takes serious time, effort, and brand willingness that doesn't fit our audience).

X seems to have the right audience, especially for PLG-to-enterprise conversion. Would need dedicated strategy and ghost-posting like LinkedIn. Alex's LinkedIn content would likely translate well to X, especially as the platform leans into articles and long-form content creation. Never got enough dedicated time to test this properly.

#### **Hacker News**

Never posted enough to get a real feel for the channel. Our top post (3 upvotes) was the Lithic/Rye agentic commerce partnership. Not sure if it is a worthwhile investment. 

#### **Newsletter**

Started because Prosus (customer) complained they didn't know what was new. Launched as a lightweight changelog recap, eventually expanded to push marketing content (YouTube videos, launches). No real strategy beyond building the muscle and maintaining a weekly touchpoint. Held steady on Tuesdays (or Wednesdays).

#### **Dev.to**

Never got around to it. Was on the list but too many other channels to maintain.

#### **What Happened When It Stopped**

Starting in November/December 2025, the team shifted focus toward long-form content (whitepapers, blogs) and launch prep. Social cadence dropped significantly as a natural result of that reprioritization.

What the data showed:
- Growth rates dropped **70-95%** across every channel
- YouTube continued growing (~23% over 3 months) under an agency, but at 3x lower rate than in-house despite similar volume (see Channel Strategy above)
- GitHub held up best organically (~24% over 3 months), carried by SEO and docs
- Every other channel slowed to single-digit percentage growth over 3 months

The takeaway isn't that the reprioritization was wrong. There were good reasons for it. The takeaway is that social distribution compounds in both directions: it builds momentum when consistent, and loses it quickly when cadence drops. That's useful to know for future prioritization decisions.

****
**Recommendation**

A few things worth keeping in mind as we ramp cadence back up:

- **Consistency matters more than any individual post:** The channels that held up during low-cadence periods (GitHub, YouTube) were the ones with compounding mechanics (SEO, algorithmic promotion). Every other channel requires regular, active distribution to grow.
- **Audience specificity matters more than volume:** YouTube continued growing under an agency at similar volume, but at 3x lower rate. The in-house content worked because it was specific to our ICP, not because there was more of it.
-** The infrastructure already exists:** Channel playbooks, voice templates, content routing guidelines, and a repeatable cadence model are all built (see `context/social-playbook.md`). Minimum viable cadence is roughly 12-15 posts/week across all accounts. At peak, we ran 20-30.

This doc will keep getting updated as we learn more from the current sprint experiments.

### Social Content Playbook

Operational reference for social content execution.


**Channels**

- LinkedIn (Alex): Founder thought leadership
- LinkedIn (Sam): CTO technical authority
- LinkedIn (Company): Brand education + product
- Reddit: Community engagement, developer trust
- Hacker News: Builder credibility, launches
- YouTube: Long-form (MCP MVP, talks, demos)
- X / Twitter: Real-time, ecosystem commentary
- TikTok: Short-form, reach beyond dev audience


**Content Routing (historical)**

When something happens, here's how to decide where it goes:

- Press hit (exec quoted): Is the quote interesting enough to stand alone? → Exec's LinkedIn (as a thought post, not a "we were quoted" post)
- Press hit (company mentioned): Is there a technical angle worth extracting? → Company LinkedIn, only if there's substance. Otherwise skip.
- New blog post: Does it teach or show something? → Company LinkedIn + X. Reddit/HN only if it's genuinely useful, not promotional.
- Product launch: Who cares about this -- devs or buyers? → Company LinkedIn (technical breakdown) + Alex LinkedIn ("why we built this") + HN (if there's something to try) + Reddit (if subreddit-appropriate)
- Someone speaking at event: Whose audience is this for? → Speaker's personal account. Company only if it's a major event.
- Video published: Is it interview, demo, or talk? → YouTube + companion blog post on Company LinkedIn + X clip
- Job posting: Leadership or IC? → Alex LinkedIn (leadership, founder energy). Company LinkedIn (IC roles).
- Partner/external request: Does our audience actually care? → Evaluate. Don't post favors.
- Breaking ecosystem news: Are we first or adding value? → X (fast take) + LinkedIn (deeper analysis next day)


**Judgment Calls (historical)**

**Not everything deserves a post**
Just because PR flagged it or someone in Slack asked "can you share this?" doesn't mean it should be posted. Ask:
1. Does our audience care about this?
2. Does this reinforce our positioning (runtime, agent infrastructure, MCP)?
3. Is there a genuine insight or value here?
If the answer to all three is no, reply to the requester and explain why it doesn't fit. Don't post out of obligation.

**"We were quoted" is not a post**
A press mention is not interesting to our audience. The insight from the quote might be. Extract it, write a standalone thought, and use the article as supporting evidence. If the quote isn't strong enough to stand on its own, skip it.

**Don't stack posts on the same account**
One post per account per day.

**Timeliness vs. quality**
- Breaking news = post fast, refine later
- Press hit from last week = take your time, craft the angle
- Event promo for next month = schedule it, don't rush

Every post should be defensible
One-sentence rationale: "This reinforces our position as the MCP runtime" or "this builds Alex's credibility as a founder who builds" or "this teaches our ICP something useful." If you can't articulate why, don't post it.

Product mention guidelines
- Do: "the runtime for MCP," "agent authorization," "agent-optimized tools"
- Don't: "API platform," "API wrapper," "lets agents call APIs"

What works vs. what doesn't
Works: Story-first posts on Alex's account. Technical breakdowns on company page. Contrarian takes from Sam. Demo videos showing a real problem being solved. Questions at the end to drive comments. Lists and whitespace for dwell time.
Doesn't: "Proud to share" / "excited to announce." Event countdown posts. Resharing press with no added POV. Short posts with no substance. Copy-pasting the same content across accounts with no voice adaptation.


**Cadence (historical)**

At peak (June-August 2025) we ran 20-30 posts/week. The cadence below is the minimum viable version to maintain channel presence and algorithm favorability.

- Alex LinkedIn: 4-5/week (thought leadership + hiring/announcements)
- Sam LinkedIn: 1-2/week (technical takes, event promos when speaking)
- Company LinkedIn: 2-3/week (technical deep dives, product/demos, ecosystem analysis)
- X: 1-2/week (real-time, shorter, more reactive)
- Reddit: 1-2/week (distribution + SEO/AIO play, lead with value)
- HN: As warranted (launches and strong technical content only)
- YouTube: Per video schedule (MCP MVP cadence + conference talks)
- TikTok: Paused (revisit when capacity allows)
- Newsletter: Weekly, Tuesday 

**Spacing rules:**
- Minimum 1 day gap between posts on the same account
- Spread related content across the week

****
**Incoming Request Handling**

When someone asks you to post something (PR agency, devrel, internal team):
1. Acknowledge -- "Got it, thanks for flagging"
2. Evaluate -- Does it fit a channel? Is there a real angle?
3. Route -- Which account, what format, when?
4. Queue -- Add to the social queue
5. Execute or decline -- If it doesn't fit, say so with a reason

Don't let requests pile up in DMs. Capture them somewhere immediately, even if you're not posting today.
